Strategic Andean Heartland

Cuzco, often referred to as the historic capital of the Inca Empire, is strategically nestled in the Andes mountains of southeastern Peru. This city, sitting at an elevation of over 3,400 meters, was once the center of the vast Incan civilization and today serves as a gateway for tourists visiting Machu Picchu and the Sacred Valley. Its location provides a stunning backdrop of rugged landscapes, with rich terrains that have supported various cultures for thousands of years. The surrounding valleys, high-altitude plains, and nearby iconic peaks like Salkantay and Ausangate contribute to its breathtaking scenery and profound sense of history.

A Confluence of Epochs

The history of Cuzco is a captivating tale of rise, fall, and rebirth, encapsulating millennia of Indigenous, Incan, and colonial narratives. Initially inhabited by the Killke people around 900 AD, the area later became the heart of the Inca Empire in the 13th century under the leadership of Manco Capac. Cuzco was designed in the shape of a puma, a sacred animal, with the fortress of Sacsayhuaman as its head. The arrival of the Spanish in the 16th century marked a period of transformation, as they erected Baroque churches and palaces atop the ruins of Incan temples, yet many of these Incan structures still stand today, testament to their engineering prowess. The city's rich tapestry of architecture and artistry, from the Coricancha temple to the colonial Cathedral, narrates the complex history of cultural convergence, resistance, and synthesis that defines Cuzco.
